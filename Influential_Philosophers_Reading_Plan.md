## Philosopher Profiles üèõÔ∏è

### Plato
- Key facts: Athenian philosopher, student of Socrates, founded the Academy.
- What he changed: Shifted philosophy toward systematic dialogue on justice, knowledge, and ideal political order.
- Main ideas: Theory of Forms, philosopher-king, tripartite soul.
- Impact: Set foundations for metaphysics, political philosophy, and educational theory.
- Important take: Build arguments by testing definitions and assumptions.
- Core reads: *Republic*, *Apology*, *Phaedo*, *Symposium*.
- Also read: *Meno*, *Gorgias*, Julia Annas, *An Introduction to Plato's Republic*.

### Aristotle
- Key facts: Plato's student, tutor to Alexander the Great, founded the Lyceum.
- What he changed: Grounded philosophy in observation, categories, causation, and formal logic.
- Main ideas: Four causes, virtue ethics (the mean), hylomorphism.
- Impact: Shaped science, ethics, rhetoric, political theory, and medieval thought.
- Important take: Character is formed by repeated action, not abstract intention alone.
- Core reads: *Nicomachean Ethics*, *Politics*, *Metaphysics*, *Poetics*.
- Also read: *Posterior Analytics*, Jonathan Lear, *Aristotle: The Desire to Understand*.

### Confucius
- Key facts: Teacher and civil advisor from Lu; teachings preserved by disciples.
- What he changed: Reframed ethics as social cultivation through ritual and moral example.
- Main ideas: Ren (humaneness), li (ritual propriety), junzi (exemplary person).
- Impact: Influenced East Asian governance, education, family ethics, and political legitimacy.
- Important take: Moral improvement is relational and continuous.
- Core reads: *Analects*.
- Also read: *Mencius*, *Xunzi*, Bryan Van Norden, *Introduction to Classical Chinese Philosophy*.

### Epicurus
- Key facts: Founded The Garden; accepted women and enslaved people in community.
- What he changed: Redefined pleasure as tranquility, not indulgence.
- Main ideas: Ataraxia, absence of pain, fear reduction (especially fear of death).
- Impact: Influenced Hellenistic ethics and modern secular well-being models.
- Important take: Simplify desires to gain freedom.
- Core reads: *Letter to Menoeceus*, *Principal Doctrines*.
- Also read: Lucretius, *On the Nature of Things*.

### Epictetus
- Key facts: Born enslaved, later Stoic teacher in Nicopolis.
- What he changed: Made Stoicism practical through the control/not-control distinction.
- Main ideas: Prohairesis (moral choice), inner freedom, disciplined judgment.
- Impact: Directly influenced CBT-like practices and resilience training.
- Important take: Focus effort only where agency exists.
- Core reads: *Enchiridion*, *Discourses*.
- Also read: Pierre Hadot, *The Inner Citadel*.

### Marcus Aurelius
- Key facts: Roman emperor writing private Stoic reflections during war and plague.
- What he changed: Modeled philosophy as daily self-governance under pressure.
- Main ideas: Impermanence, duty, universal reason, self-command.
- Impact: Enduring model for ethical leadership and personal discipline.
- Important take: Practice philosophy in ordinary decisions.
- Core reads: *Meditations*.
- Also read: Donald Robertson, *How to Think Like a Roman Emperor*.

### Plotinus
- Key facts: Major Neoplatonist; teachings compiled as *Enneads*.
- What he changed: Built a metaphysical hierarchy from The One to intellect to soul.
- Main ideas: Emanation, return to the One, contemplative ascent.
- Impact: Deep influence on Christian, Islamic, and Jewish metaphysics.
- Important take: Intellectual and moral purification are linked.
- Core reads: *Enneads* (selections).
- Also read: Lloyd Gerson, *Plotinus*.

### Augustine of Hippo
- Key facts: North African bishop; converted from Manichaeism to Christianity.
- What he changed: United classical philosophy with Christian theology and introspection.
- Main ideas: Restless heart, grace, time and memory, two cities.
- Impact: Defined major lines of Western theology and philosophy of self.
- Important take: Interior life is philosophically serious.
- Core reads: *Confessions*, *City of God*.
- Also read: *On Free Choice of the Will*.

### Avicenna (Ibn Sina)
- Key facts: Persian polymath in medicine, logic, and metaphysics.
- What he changed: Distinguished essence from existence in a rigorous way.
- Main ideas: Necessary Existent, floating man argument, demonstrative science.
- Impact: Central to medieval Islamic and Latin scholastic thought.
- Important take: Clarify what a thing is before claiming that it is.
- Core reads: *The Book of Healing* (metaphysics selections).
- Also read: Peter Adamson, *Philosophy in the Islamic World* (sections on Avicenna).

### Maimonides
- Key facts: Jewish philosopher, legal scholar, and physician in Egypt.
- What he changed: Balanced revealed religion with Aristotelian rationalism.
- Main ideas: Negative theology, intellectual perfection, law and ethics.
- Impact: Lasting influence in Jewish thought and cross-tradition medieval debates.
- Important take: Precision in language protects theology from confusion.
- Core reads: *Guide for the Perplexed*.
- Also read: *Mishneh Torah* (ethical/legal selections).

### Thomas Aquinas
- Key facts: Dominican theologian and scholastic system-builder.
- What he changed: Integrated Aristotle into Christian doctrine at scale.
- Main ideas: Natural law, analogy of being, faith and reason harmony.
- Impact: Cornerstone of Catholic intellectual tradition and legal ethics.
- Important take: Reason and revelation can cooperate without collapsing into each other.
- Core reads: *Summa Theologiae* (selected questions), *Summa contra Gentiles*.
- Also read: Brian Davies, *The Thought of Thomas Aquinas*.

### Rene Descartes
- Key facts: French mathematician-philosopher and methodological skeptic.
- What he changed: Recentered epistemology on certainty and method.
- Main ideas: Cogito, clear and distinct ideas, mind-body dualism.
- Impact: Opened modern debates in epistemology, consciousness, and science.
- Important take: Doubt can be a method for stronger knowledge.
- Core reads: *Discourse on Method*, *Meditations on First Philosophy*.
- Also read: *Principles of Philosophy*.

### Thomas Hobbes
- Key facts: English philosopher shaped by civil war politics.
- What he changed: Framed political legitimacy through social contract and security.
- Main ideas: State of nature, sovereign authority, fear and order.
- Impact: Foundation for modern political realism and state theory.
- Important take: Political order solves coordination and violence problems first.
- Core reads: *Leviathan*.
- Also read: Quentin Skinner, *Hobbes and Republican Liberty*.

### Baruch Spinoza
- Key facts: Lens grinder and philosopher excommunicated from his community.
- What he changed: Proposed a radical monism where God and Nature are one substance.
- Main ideas: Substance monism, conatus, emotions as intelligible processes.
- Impact: Influenced secularism, biblical criticism, and modern naturalism.
- Important take: Understanding causes can transform emotional life.
- Core reads: *Ethics*, *Theological-Political Treatise*.
- Also read: Steven Nadler, *Spinoza: A Life*.

### John Locke
- Key facts: Physician-philosopher tied to liberal constitutional debates.
- What he changed: Developed empiricism and rights-based political legitimacy.
- Main ideas: Tabula rasa, consent of governed, property rights.
- Impact: Influenced liberal democracies and constitutional thought.
- Important take: Political power needs justification from persons, not lineage.
- Core reads: *Essay Concerning Human Understanding*, *Two Treatises of Government*.
- Also read: *A Letter Concerning Toleration*.

### David Hume
- Key facts: Scottish Enlightenment skeptic and historian.
- What he changed: Challenged certainty about causation, self, and induction.
- Main ideas: Habit-based belief, is/ought gap, bundle theory of self.
- Impact: Triggered Kant's response and shaped analytic empiricism.
- Important take: Human psychology sets limits on reason.
- Core reads: *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*, *Treatise of Human Nature* (Book 1 selections).
- Also read: *Dialogues Concerning Natural Religion*.

### Jean-Jacques Rousseau
- Key facts: Genevan thinker critical of inequality and corrupt social institutions.
- What he changed: Recast freedom as political self-legislation and moral education.
- Main ideas: General will, social inequality critique, civic formation.
- Impact: Influenced republicanism, revolution, and modern educational theory.
- Important take: Institutions can deform or develop human freedom.
- Core reads: *Discourse on Inequality*, *The Social Contract*, *Emile*.
- Also read: Judith Shklar, *Men and Citizens*.

### Immanuel Kant
- Key facts: Lived and taught in Konigsberg; transformed modern philosophy.
- What he changed: Synthesized rationalism and empiricism via transcendental idealism.
- Main ideas: Conditions of experience, categorical imperative, autonomy.
- Impact: Structured modern ethics, epistemology, aesthetics, and political theory.
- Important take: Freedom and obligation are internally linked.
- Core reads: *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*, *Critique of Pure Reason* (selected).
- Also read: *Critique of Practical Reason*, Allen Wood, *Kant*.

### G.W.F. Hegel
- Key facts: German idealist focused on history, social institutions, and reason.
- What he changed: Made historical development central to philosophical explanation.
- Main ideas: Dialectical development, recognition, ethical life (Sittlichkeit).
- Impact: Shaped Marxism, existentialism, critical theory, and social philosophy.
- Important take: Individual freedom depends on social forms.
- Core reads: *Phenomenology of Spirit* (Preface + selected chapters), *Philosophy of Right*.
- Also read: Robert Pippin, *Hegel's Idealism*.

### Soren Kierkegaard
- Key facts: Danish writer using pseudonyms to stage existential choices.
- What he changed: Shifted focus from system to lived subjectivity and commitment.
- Main ideas: Anxiety, leap of faith, stages of life.
- Impact: Major source for existentialism and theology of inwardness.
- Important take: Truth can be existentially appropriated, not only theoretically known.
- Core reads: *Either/Or* (selections), *Fear and Trembling*.
- Also read: *The Sickness Unto Death*.

### John Stuart Mill
- Key facts: Public intellectual and parliamentarian in Victorian Britain.
- What he changed: Refined utilitarianism and defended liberty against social tyranny.
- Main ideas: Harm principle, qualitative pleasures, individuality.
- Impact: Enduring influence in liberal law, ethics, and free speech debates.
- Important take: Protect dissent as a social good.
- Core reads: *On Liberty*, *Utilitarianism*.
- Also read: *The Subjection of Women*.

### Karl Marx
- Key facts: German philosopher-economist working largely in exile.
- What he changed: Linked philosophy to historical material analysis of capitalism.
- Main ideas: Alienation, class struggle, surplus value.
- Impact: Reshaped political economy, labor politics, and critical social theory.
- Important take: Material structures shape consciousness and institutions.
- Core reads: *Communist Manifesto*, *Capital* Vol. 1 (selected chapters).
- Also read: *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844*, David Harvey's *Companion to Marx's Capital*.

### Friedrich Nietzsche
- Key facts: Philologist turned cultural critic; wrote in aphoristic style.
- What he changed: Critiqued morality as historically produced rather than timeless.
- Main ideas: Genealogy, will to power, nihilism, revaluation of values.
- Impact: Influenced existentialism, psychoanalysis, post-structuralism.
- Important take: Examine the origin and function of your values.
- Core reads: *Beyond Good and Evil*, *On the Genealogy of Morality*.
- Also read: *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, Walter Kaufmann, *Nietzsche*.

### Gottlob Frege
- Key facts: Mathematician-logician who transformed semantics and logic.
- What he changed: Introduced predicate logic and a new theory of meaning.
- Main ideas: Sense/reference distinction, anti-psychologism, logicism.
- Impact: Foundation of analytic philosophy and philosophy of language.
- Important take: Formal clarity can reveal hidden assumptions in ordinary language.
- Core reads: *Begriffsschrift*, "On Sense and Reference".
- Also read: "The Thought".

### Bertrand Russell
- Key facts: Philosopher, logician, public intellectual, and Nobel laureate.
- What he changed: Advanced logical analysis as a method for philosophy.
- Main ideas: Logical atomism, descriptions theory, epistemic analysis.
- Impact: Co-founded analytic tradition and modern logic pedagogy.
- Important take: Reduce big metaphysical claims to analyzable propositions.
- Core reads: *The Problems of Philosophy*, "On Denoting".
- Also read: *Principia Mathematica* (with Whitehead, selected sections).

### Ludwig Wittgenstein
- Key facts: Austrian-British philosopher with two major philosophical phases.
- What he changed: Moved from logical picture theory to language-use pragmatics.
- Main ideas: Language games, forms of life, meaning as use.
- Impact: Reoriented philosophy of language, mind, and method.
- Important take: Many philosophical problems are misuse-of-language problems.
- Core reads: *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus*, *Philosophical Investigations*.
- Also read: *On Certainty*, James Conant and Cora Diamond essays.

## 6-Month Intensive Plan üöÄ

Use this if you can commit ~8-10 hours/week.

### Months 1-2: Foundations
- Plato, Aristotle, Confucius.
- Goal: map ethics, politics, and knowledge vocabulary.
- Output: one 2-page comparison memo each month.

### Months 3-4: Medieval to Early Modern
- Augustine, Aquinas, Descartes, Hobbes, Locke, Hume, Spinoza.
- Goal: understand faith/reason and rationalism/empiricism split.
- Output: one argument map per thinker.

### Month 5: Enlightenment and Idealism
- Rousseau, Kant, Hegel.
- Goal: connect freedom, autonomy, and historical reason.
- Output: short essay: "What is freedom?"

### Month 6: Modern and Analytic Pivot
- Kierkegaard, Mill, Marx, Nietzsche, Frege, Russell, Wittgenstein.
- Goal: compare existential, political-economic, and language turns.
- Output: final synthesis paper (6-10 pages).

## 12-Month Deep Plan üóìÔ∏è

Use this if you can commit ~5-7 hours/week.

### Quarter 1 (Months 1-3)
- Plato, Aristotle, Confucius, Epicurus, Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius.
- Focus: virtue, flourishing, self-mastery.

### Quarter 2 (Months 4-6)
- Plotinus, Augustine, Avicenna, Maimonides, Aquinas.
- Focus: metaphysics, theology, and reason.

### Quarter 3 (Months 7-9)
- Descartes, Hobbes, Locke, Hume, Spinoza, Rousseau.
- Focus: self, state, knowledge, legitimacy.

### Quarter 4 (Months 10-12)
- Kant, Hegel, Kierkegaard, Mill, Marx, Nietzsche, Frege, Russell, Wittgenstein.
- Focus: modernity, critique, and analytic method.

### Monthly cadence (repeat each month)
- Week 1: primary text reading.
- Week 2: secondary commentary and notes cleanup.
- Week 3: comparative writing (2-3 pages).
- Week 4: discussion, revision, and spaced review.

## How To Study This Well ‚úÖ

- Keep a glossary for recurring terms (substance, autonomy, dialectic, utility, etc.).
- Write one "steelman" and one "critique" for each thinker.
- Pair each philosopher with one modern problem (AI ethics, democracy, inequality, meaning).
- Revisit hard texts twice: first for structure, second for argument details.
